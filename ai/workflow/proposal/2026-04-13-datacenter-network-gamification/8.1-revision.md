# Revision Notes for 7-final.md

Analysis of which paragraphs contain more detail than a simple proposal requires. The proposal format calls for roughly 1/3 to 1/2 page per section -- many paragraphs below exceed that by including implementation-depth detail better saved for the project report.

## Section 1: Introduction (target: ~1/2 to 1/3 page)

**Paragraph 1 (line 5)** -- Good length and density. Keep as-is. Sets up the problem concisely.

**Paragraph 2 (line 7)** -- Good. Keep as-is. Establishes the educational gap.

**Paragraph 3 (line 9)** -- Too detailed for a proposal. The concrete example is good but goes too deep into the mechanics (24 sequential hops, 400 Gbps links, PFLOPS math). Trim to 2-3 sentences: state that a ring bottlenecks while a fat-tree doesn't, and that the trade-off is invisible in textbook diagrams. Cut the specific numbers.

**Line 11 (research question)** -- Keep as-is.

**Overall**: Section 1 is close to the right length. Trimming paragraph 3 brings it to ~1/3 page.

## Section 2: State of the Art (target: ~1/2 page)

**Paragraph 2 (line 17, topology research)** -- Too much per-paper detail. For a proposal, each paper needs one sentence, not two. Cut: "scaling to 27,648 hosts," "cutting cost by 20-52% compared to alternatives at scale," "while enabling incremental expansion," "scaling 100x to over 1 Pbps of bisection bandwidth." Keep: the name, the core idea, and one distinguishing fact per paper. The closing sentence ("These papers are rigorous...") is good -- keep it.

**Paragraph 3 (line 19, network simulation)** -- Too much detail. The specific performance numbers (305 million events/sec, 16,384 cores, 744x speedup, under 1% error) are interesting but unnecessary for a proposal. Reduce each tool to one sentence: ns-3 is the gold standard but heavyweight; FatTreeSim and Wormhole show DES works for this domain at scale. Cut the numbers.

**Paragraph 4 (line 21, educational gamification)** -- Good length. Keep as-is.

**Paragraph 5 (line 23, gap statement)** -- Good. Keep as-is. This is the payoff of the section.

**Overall**: Section 2 is the most over-length. Trimming paragraphs 2 and 3 brings it to ~1/2 page.

## Section 3: Proposed Solution (target: ~1/3 page or more)

**Paragraph 1 (line 27, core insight)** -- Good. Keep as-is.

**Paragraphs 2-4 (lines 31-35, three components)** -- Each component paragraph is individually fine, but collectively they are too long for a 1/3-page section. Options:
- Merge all three into a single paragraph with one sentence per component.
- Or keep the three-paragraph structure but cut the second sentence of each (the elaboration). Keep only the first sentence that names the component and its role.

**Paragraph 5 (line 37, feasibility/implementation)** -- Too detailed. The ns-3 comparison, the 300-links calculation, the "millions of events per second" claim, and the full tech stack enumeration (HTML/CSS/JS, Canvas 2D, Three.js from CDN) are implementation details. For the proposal, reduce to: "The simulation is computationally trivial at 25 nodes and runs entirely client-side in a browser with no installation required."

**Milestones (lines 41-48)** -- Good structure and length. Keep as-is. These are explicitly requested by the proposal format ("concrete milestones or outline your solution step-by-step").

**Overall**: Section 3 is the most over-length section. The three-component description and the feasibility paragraph need significant trimming. The milestones stay.
