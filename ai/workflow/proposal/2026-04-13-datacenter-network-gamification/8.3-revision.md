## Section 1: Introduction

**Paragraph 1 (line 5)** -- Still reads like an academic paper, not a proposal. Problems:
- "one of the most consequential decisions in high-performance computing infrastructure, yet it remains one of the least intuitive" -- wordy. Just say what the problem is.
- The parenthetical listing of topology names (ring, mesh, fat-tree, torus, dragonfly, Clos) is unnecessary here -- the reader doesn't need the full menu yet.
- "the all-reduce communication pattern (where every node must share its computed gradients with every other node)" -- the parenthetical explanation is jargon explaining jargon. Either use plain language or drop the term entirely.
- "Communication overhead can consume 30-50% of total training time" -- good concrete fact, keep it, but the sentence around it is too formal.

**Suggested rewrite for paragraph 1**: How you wire together the computers in a datacenter matters. A lot. A bad layout means expensive hardware sits idle, waiting on data that is stuck in transit. In distributed AI training, every node has to share its results with every other node. If the network can't keep up, 30-50% of total training time gets burned just moving data around.

**Paragraph 2 (line 7)** -- Problems:
- "graph-theoretic properties of a network" -- jargon that can be simplified.
- "they memorize that a fat-tree has full bisection bandwidth" -- this assumes the reader knows what bisection bandwidth is. At this point in the proposal, they might not.
- The ring vs fat-tree example is good but still slightly technical ("serial chain of hops"). Simplify.

**Suggested rewrite for paragraph 2**: Students never get to try this themselves. They see network diagrams in a textbook. They memorize which ones are "good." But they never actually build a network and watch it work. Or fail.

Here is a simple example. Take 25 computers. Wire them in a ring. Now every piece of data has to hop through all 25 nodes, one at a time. Slow. Wire them as a tree instead and data flows in parallel. Fast. That difference is obvious once you see it. But you cannot see it in a diagram.

**Research question (line 9)** -- Keep as-is. It's already clear and concise.

## Section 2: State of the Art

**Paragraph 1 (line 13, "Existing work falls...")** -- Fine. Keep.

**Paragraph 2 (line 15, topology research)** -- Problems:
- "full bisection bandwidth" -- jargon, unexplained.
- "reducing network diameter to three hops via hierarchical groups of high-radix routers" -- too technical for a proposal. The reader doesn't need the mechanism.
- "These papers are rigorous but assume expert readers -- they do not build intuition" -- good closing, keep it.

**Suggested rewrite**: Researchers have proposed several datacenter network designs. Al-Fares et al. [1] introduced the fat-tree, which uses cheap off-the-shelf switches to maximize throughput. Kim et al. [2] proposed the dragonfly, which cuts down the number of hops data has to travel. Singla et al. [3] showed that even random wiring patterns can beat carefully engineered ones. Singh et al. [4] documented how Google built and evolved its datacenter networks over a decade. All of these papers are written for experts. None of them try to build intuition.

**Paragraph 3 (line 17, network simulation)** -- Problems:
- "packet-level fidelity" -- can be simplified.
- "heavyweight research instruments requiring significant expertise and infrastructure" -- a bit wordy.

**Suggested rewrite**: Network simulators exist. ns-3 [5], FatTreeSim [6], and Wormhole [7] all let researchers test how a network design performs by simulating traffic in detail. They work. But they are research tools. They take real expertise to set up. They are not built for learning.

**Paragraph 4 (line 19, educational gamification)** -- Mostly fine but slightly wordy.

**Suggested rewrite**: There are educational tools. Conquer the Net [8] and NetEmulator [9] both teach networking through interactive exercises. But they focus on configuring individual devices. Setting IP addresses. Filling in routing tables. Neither one lets you design the network itself. And neither one shows you what happens to performance when you do.

**Paragraph 5 (line 21, gap statement)** -- Good. Simplify slightly.

**Suggested rewrite**: That leaves a gap. Nobody has built a tool that lets you design a network, run traffic through it, and see how your choices affect performance.
