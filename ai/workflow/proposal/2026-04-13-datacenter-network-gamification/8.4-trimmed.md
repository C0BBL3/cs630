# An Analysis and Gamification of Networking in Data Centers

## 1. Introduction

How you wire together the computers in a datacenter matters. A lot. A bad layout means expensive hardware sits idle, waiting on data that is stuck in transit. In distributed AI training, every node has to share its results with every other node. If the network can't keep up, 30-50% of total training time gets burned just moving data around.

Students never get to try this themselves. They see network diagrams in a textbook. They memorize which ones are "good." But they never actually build a network and watch it work. Or fail.

Here is a simple example. Take 25 computers. Wire them in a ring. Now every piece of data has to hop through all 25 nodes, one at a time. Slow. Wire them as a tree instead and data flows in parallel. Fast. That difference is obvious once you see it. But you cannot see it in a diagram.

**Research question:** Can a gamified, interactive network topology designer, where users build datacenter interconnects under realistic constraints and receive immediate performance feedback, teach the relationship between network structure and distributed computing performance?

## 2. State of the Art

Existing work in this space falls into three categories. None of them overlap.

Researchers have proposed several datacenter network designs. Al-Fares et al. [1] introduced the fat-tree, which uses cheap off-the-shelf switches to maximize throughput. Kim et al. [2] proposed the dragonfly, which cuts down the number of hops data has to travel. Singla et al. [3] showed that even random wiring patterns can beat carefully engineered ones. Singh et al. [4] documented how Google built and evolved its datacenter networks over a decade. All of these papers are written for experts. None of them try to build intuition.

Network simulators exist. ns-3 [5], FatTreeSim [6], and Wormhole [7] all let researchers test how a network design performs by simulating traffic in detail. They work. But they are research tools. They take real expertise to set up. They are not built for learning.

There are educational tools. Conquer the Net [8] and NetEmulator [9] both teach networking through interactive exercises. But they focus on configuring individual devices. Setting IP addresses. Filling in routing tables. Neither one lets you design the network itself. And neither one shows you what happens to performance when you do.

That leaves a gap. Nobody has built a tool that lets you design a network, run traffic through it, and see how your choices affect performance.

## 3. Proposed Solution

The core insight is simple. You learn network design by building networks and watching them fail. When you connect nodes in a ring and watch packets pile up at a bottleneck while your score drops, you understand why that layout is bad. When you add one extra link and your score jumps, you understand why it helps. No lecture can do that.

We are building a browser-based game with three pieces. First, an interactive editor where you place nodes and draw connections. Early levels lock nodes to a 2D grid so you focus on wiring. Later levels let you place nodes freely. The final levels go full 3D. Second, a simulation engine that models real traffic. Every link has bandwidth and delay. Packets queue up at congested links just like they would in a real network. The engine tries multiple communication strategies and scores your design based on the best one. Scores are deterministic and identical across every browser and platform. Third, a level system. Eight levels, starting with 4 unconstrained nodes and ending with 25 nodes under every constraint at once: connection limits, cable length, total cable budget, planarity. Each level you complete unlocks an encyclopedia entry about a real datacenter topology, showing how your design compares.

The whole thing runs in a browser. No install. No backend. Open one HTML file and play.

### Milestones

1. Core graph model: node/edge data structures, shortest path, diameter, bisection bandwidth.
2. Simulation engine: packet-level discrete-event simulation, ring and tree all-reduce, scoring formula.
3. 2D editor and levels 1-3: fixed-position editor, constraint validation, first playable levels.
4. Scoring visualization: animated packet flow, bottleneck highlighting, score breakdown, star rating.
5. Advanced levels 4-6: free placement, cable budget, planarity constraints.
6. 3D mode and levels 7-8: Three.js integration, 3D editor.
7. Encyclopedia and polish: unlockable topology entries, tutorial overlays, UI polish.
8. Academic paper: system design analysis, topology analysis, results.
