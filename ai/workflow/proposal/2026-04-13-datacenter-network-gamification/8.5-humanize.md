# Humanization Notes for 8.4-trimmed.md

The current draft over-corrected from academic stiffness into a choppy, staccato style. Too many sentence fragments. Too many one-word sentences used for dramatic effect ("Slow." "Fast." "A lot."). It reads like ad copy or a pitch deck, not like a person explaining their project. The goal is conversational and natural, the way you'd describe this to a classmate or your advisor in an email.

## Section 1: Introduction

**Paragraph 1 (line 5)**: "How you wire together the computers in a datacenter matters. A lot." The fragment "A lot." is trying too hard. Combine into one sentence that flows naturally.

**Suggested rewrite**: The way computers are wired together inside a datacenter has a huge impact on how well they perform. A bad network layout can leave expensive hardware sitting idle while data is stuck waiting to move between machines. In distributed AI training, for instance, every node needs to share its results with every other node, and if the network becomes the bottleneck, 30-50% of total training time can be lost just to communication.

**Paragraph 2 (line 7)**: "They see network diagrams in a textbook. They memorize which ones are 'good.' But they never actually build a network and watch it work. Or fail." The repeated short sentences and the "Or fail." fragment feel rehearsed. Let it flow more naturally.

**Suggested rewrite**: Despite how much this matters, students rarely get hands-on experience with network design. They see diagrams in a textbook and memorize which topologies are considered good, but they never get to build one themselves and see how it actually performs under real traffic.

**Paragraph 3 (line 9)**: "Take 25 computers. Wire them in a ring. Now every piece of data has to hop through all 25 nodes, one at a time. Slow. Wire them as a tree instead and data flows in parallel. Fast." The one-word sentences "Slow." and "Fast." are the most artificial-sounding parts of the whole document. Fold them into real sentences.

**Suggested rewrite**: Consider a simple example: if you take 25 computers and wire them in a ring, every piece of data has to hop through all 25 nodes one at a time, which creates a massive bottleneck. If you wire them as a tree instead, data can flow in parallel and the same job finishes much faster. That difference is easy to understand once you see it happen, but it's nearly impossible to appreciate from a static diagram.

## Section 2: State of the Art

**Paragraph 1 (line 15)**: "None of them overlap." feels abrupt on its own. Fold into the previous sentence.

**Suggested rewrite for opening**: Existing work in this space falls into three categories that don't really overlap.

**Paragraph 2 (line 17)**: "All of these papers are written for experts. None of them try to build intuition." Two short declarative sentences in a row with the same structure. Combine.

**Suggested rewrite for closing**: These papers are foundational, but they're written for experts and don't try to build intuition about why these designs work.

**Paragraph 3 (line 19)**: "Network simulators exist." as an opener is too blunt. "They work. But they are research tools. They take real expertise to set up. They are not built for learning." is four sentences that could be two.

**Suggested rewrite**: On the simulation side, tools like ns-3 [5], FatTreeSim [6], and Wormhole [7] let researchers test network designs by simulating traffic in detail. They're effective, but they're research-grade tools that require significant expertise to set up and aren't designed for learning.

**Paragraph 4 (line 21)**: "Setting IP addresses. Filling in routing tables." Sentence fragments used for rhythm again. Fold into a normal sentence.

**Suggested rewrite**: There are also educational tools like Conquer the Net [8] and NetEmulator [9] that teach networking through interactive exercises, but they focus on configuring individual devices like setting IP addresses and routing tables. Neither one lets you design the network layout itself or shows you how topology affects overall performance.

**Paragraph 5 (line 23)**: "Nobody has built a tool..." is fine but "That leaves a gap." as a standalone sentence is a bit blunt. Combine.

**Suggested rewrite**: That leaves a clear gap: there is no tool that lets you design a network, run traffic through it, and see how your design choices affect performance.

## Section 3: Proposed Solution

**Paragraph 1 (line 27)**: "The core insight is simple." followed by "No lecture can do that." Both feel like they're performing rather than explaining. Soften.

**Suggested rewrite**: The idea behind this project is that you learn network design by building networks and watching them succeed or fail. When you connect nodes in a ring and see packets pile up at a bottleneck while your score drops, you start to understand why that layout doesn't work. When you add one extra link and your score jumps, you see why it helps. That kind of feedback is hard to get from a lecture.

**Paragraph 2 (line 29)**: This is the densest paragraph in the document. The "First... Second... Third..." structure is clear but the paragraph is very long. Break it into three short paragraphs, one per component, without resorting to fragments.

**Paragraph 3 (line 31)**: "The whole thing runs in a browser. No install. No backend. Open one HTML file and play." Back to staccato fragments. Combine into one natural sentence.

**Suggested rewrite**: The entire application runs in a browser with no installation and no backend required. You just open a single HTML file and start playing.
