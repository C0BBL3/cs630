# An Analysis and Gamification of Networking in Data Centers

## 1. Introduction

Datacenter network topology is one of the most consequential decisions in high-performance computing infrastructure, yet it remains one of the least intuitive. The choice of how to interconnect compute nodes -- ring, mesh, fat-tree, torus, dragonfly, Clos -- directly determines the effective throughput of distributed workloads. In modern AI training, the all-reduce communication pattern (where every node must share its computed gradients with every other node) means that a poor interconnect can idle expensive hardware while data waits in transit. Communication overhead can consume 30-50% of total training time in large distributed jobs, and the interconnect topology is the primary lever for reducing it.

Despite this, the relationship between graph-theoretic properties of a network and real workload performance is rarely taught interactively. Students learn these topologies as static diagrams -- they memorize that a fat-tree has full bisection bandwidth, but they never experience *why* by building a topology and watching it succeed or fail under load. For example, connecting 25 compute nodes as a ring forces all-reduce traffic through a serial chain of hops, bottlenecking performance -- while a fat-tree over the same nodes avoids this, at the cost of more cabling. The trade-off is real, but invisible in a textbook.

**Research question:** Can a gamified, interactive network topology designer -- where users build datacenter interconnects under realistic constraints and receive immediate performance feedback -- serve as an effective tool for teaching the relationship between graph-theoretic network properties and distributed computing performance?

## 2. State of the Art

Existing work falls into three categories that do not overlap.

**Topology research** has produced foundational datacenter network designs. Al-Fares et al. [1] proposed the fat-tree using commodity switches to achieve full bisection bandwidth. Kim et al. [2] introduced the dragonfly, reducing network diameter to three hops via hierarchical groups of high-radix routers. Singla et al. [3] showed that random graph topologies (Jellyfish) can outperform structured designs. Singh et al. [4] documented Google's decade of multi-stage Clos networks at production scale. These papers are rigorous but assume expert readers -- they do not build intuition.

**Network simulation** tools like ns-3 [5], FatTreeSim [6], and Wormhole [7] enable researchers to evaluate topology performance at packet-level fidelity. All three demonstrate that discrete-event simulation is effective for datacenter topologies and all-reduce workloads. However, they are heavyweight research instruments requiring significant expertise and infrastructure -- none is designed for interactive or educational use.

**Educational gamification** tools like Conquer the Net [8] and NetEmulator [9] teach networking interactively. Conquer the Net gamifies device configuration (IP addressing, routing tables). NetEmulator provides a web-based drag-and-drop topology builder with real-time packet visualization. Both focus on protocol configuration, not topology design -- neither connects topology structure to aggregate performance metrics.

The gap: no existing tool lets users design datacenter network topologies under realistic constraints and receive immediate feedback on how their design affects distributed workload performance.

## 3. Proposed Solution

The core insight is that the relationship between network topology and performance is best understood by building and failing. When a user connects nodes in a ring and watches packets queue at a bottleneck while their score drops, they learn why bisection bandwidth matters. When they add a cross-link and see their score jump, they discover the power of reducing diameter firsthand.

The solution is a browser-based game with three components: (1) an interactive network editor that progresses from fixed 2D grids to free-placement 2D to full 3D across levels, (2) a deterministic packet-level discrete-event simulation engine that models all-reduce communication with congestion and queuing, producing consistent cross-platform scores via seeded randomness, and (3) a progressive level system with 8 levels ramping from 4 unconstrained nodes to 25 nodes with all constraints active (max degree, cable length, cable budget, planarity), each unlocking an encyclopedia entry about a real-world topology.

The simulation is computationally trivial at 25 nodes and runs entirely client-side in a browser with no installation required.
