# Problem Space

## Published Work

### Datacenter Network Topologies

**Al-Fares, M., Loukissas, A., and Vahdat, A. "A Scalable, Commodity Data Center Network Architecture." ACM SIGCOMM 2008, pp. 63-74.** https://www.scirp.org/(S(czeh4tfqyw2orz553k1w0r45))/reference/referencespapers?referenceid=2006753
The seminal fat-tree paper. Addresses the problem that traditional hierarchical datacenter networks using expensive high-end switches at higher tiers supported only 50% of aggregate edge bandwidth. Proposes a folded Clos (fat-tree) topology built entirely from commodity Ethernet switches that delivers full bisection bandwidth -- any host can communicate with any other host at the full speed of its network interface. A 48-port switch configuration scales to 27,648 hosts without requiring specialized hardware or host modifications. Directly relevant: the fat-tree is the foundational topology the game's encyclopedia will teach, and its full bisection bandwidth makes it a natural benchmark for scoring player designs. Strength: practical, backward-compatible with existing IP/Ethernet, and proven at scale. Limitation for our purposes: the paper presents the topology for an expert audience; a reader must already understand bisection bandwidth to appreciate why the design works.

**Kim, J., Dally, W.J., Scott, S., and Abts, D. "Technology-Driven, Highly-Scalable Dragonfly Topology." ISCA 2008, pp. 77-88.** https://www.cs.umd.edu/class/spring2021/cmsc714/student-slides/XW-1.pdf
Introduces a hierarchical three-level topology (router, group, system) where groups of high-radix routers act as virtual routers, reducing network diameter to three hops and minimizing the number of expensive global (inter-cabinet) cables. At 16K+ nodes, the dragonfly reduces cost by 20% compared to a flattened butterfly and 52% compared to a folded Clos. The paper also introduces two global adaptive routing variants (UGAL-L with selective virtual-channel discrimination and credit round-trip latency sensing) that achieve near-ideal throughput and latency. Relevant as an advanced topology where the design trade-off -- fewer global cables but more complex routing -- is exactly the kind of constraint the game's later levels will force players to confront. Limitation: the structured group design limits bandwidth and resiliency under adversarial traffic patterns.

**Singla, A., Hong, C.-Y., Popa, L., and Godfrey, P.B. "Jellyfish: Networking Data Centers Randomly." NSDI 2012, pp. 225-238.** https://experts.illinois.edu/en/publications/jellyfish-networking-data-centers-randomly/
Challenges the assumption that datacenter networks must follow structured topologies. By adopting a random regular graph topology, Jellyfish supports 25% more servers at full capacity than fat-trees using identical equipment, and the advantage grows with scale. Crucially, it yields itself naturally to incremental expansion -- a major practical concern since industry experience shows datacenters rarely deploy all capacity at once. The paper addresses the new challenges random graphs introduce (routing complexity, physical layout, wiring) and demonstrates feasible solutions. Relevant as a counterpoint to structured designs: players who experiment with unstructured connections may accidentally approach Jellyfish-like performance, and the encyclopedia can teach them why random graphs can outperform engineered ones.

**Singh, A., et al. "Jupiter Rising: A Decade of Clos Topologies and Centralized Control in Google's Datacenter Network." ACM SIGCOMM 2015.** https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2975159
Documents five generations of Google's datacenter networks, unified by three principles: multi-stage Clos topologies built from commodity merchant silicon, centralized control (global configuration pushed to all switches rather than decentralized routing protocols), and modular hardware/software design. Over a decade, these networks scaled 100x to more than 1 Pbps of bisection bandwidth, with bandwidth demands doubling every 12-15 months. Relevant as the definitive industry case study demonstrating that topology choices at hyperscaler scale translate directly into cost, capacity, and operational complexity. Strength: concrete, production-validated evidence that the problem the game addresses matters enormously in practice. Limitation: focuses on operational and engineering lessons at scales far beyond the game's 25-node scope, and does not address education or intuition-building.

### Network Simulation

**ns-3 (nsnam.org).** https://nsnam.org
The standard open-source discrete-event network simulator (current release: ns-3.47). Implemented in C++ with a Python API, it provides packet-level simulation with protocol-level fidelity across a wide range of network types. Relevant as the gold standard for network simulation whose architecture (event queue, per-link queuing models, modular protocol stacks) directly informs our lightweight browser-based DES engine. Strength: comprehensive, validated, and widely used in both research and education. Limitation: heavyweight -- requires C++ compilation, platform-specific installation, and significant expertise to configure. Not suitable for a browser-based educational tool, but establishes the design patterns we adapt.

**Liu, N., Haider, A., Jin, D., and Sun, X.-H. "Modeling and Simulation of Extreme-Scale Fat-Tree Networks for HPC Systems and Data Centers." ACM Trans. Model. Comput. Simul. 27(2), 2017.** https://publish.illinois.edu/science-of-security-lablet/files/2014/05/A-Modeling-and-Simulation-of-Extreme-Scale-Fat-Tree-Networks-for-HPC-Systems-and-Data-Centers.pdf
A parallel discrete-event simulation (PDES) toolkit built on the ROSS and CODES frameworks, designed specifically for fat-tree networks at extreme scale. FatTreeSim achieved a peak performance of 305 million events per second on 16,384 cores of Blue Gene/Q and was validated by simulating large-scale Hadoop YARN applications. Relevant as evidence that discrete-event simulation is an established and effective approach for evaluating fat-tree datacenter topologies. Strength: demonstrates that the event-driven simulation model works for this problem domain at scale. Limitation: designed for HPC research on supercomputers, not for interactive or educational use.

**Long, F., et al. "Supercharging Packet-level Network Simulation of Large Model Training via Memoization and Fast-Forwarding." arXiv:2602.10615, 2026.** https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.10615
Proposes Wormhole, a PLDES kernel that exploits the observation that distributed LLM training traffic exhibits repetitive contention patterns and steady-states where flow rates stabilize. By automatically memoizing unsteady-states and fast-forwarding through steady-states, Wormhole achieves 744x speedup over ns-3 (510x for MoE workloads) with bounded error under 1%, reducing simulation time for GPT-13B training on 128 GPUs from 9 hours to 5 minutes. Relevant as direct evidence that packet-level simulation of all-reduce workloads is both well-studied and tractable with the right optimizations. Limitation: targets production-scale training jobs with hundreds of GPUs; our 25-node scope is small enough that raw DES without such optimizations will complete in milliseconds.

### Gamification in CS Education

**Arevalillo-Herráez, M., Morán-Gómez, R., and Claver, J.M. "Conquer the Net: An Educational Computer Game to Learn the Basic Configuration of Networking Components." Computer Applications in Engineering Education, 20(1), 2012, pp. 72-77.** https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/cae.20374
Developed at the University of Valencia to address the problem that advanced networking equipment is expensive and student access is limited to scheduled lab sessions. The game provides a motivating environment for practicing basic networking configuration commands (IP addressing, routing tables, interface setup) with simple rules combining cooperative and competitive learning scenarios. Relevant as the closest existing work to our project in spirit -- gamified networking education. Strength: demonstrates that game-based learning is effective and engaging for networking concepts outside formal lab time. Limitation: focuses entirely on device configuration tasks; players do not design the network topology or see how topology choices affect aggregate throughput or distributed workload performance.

**NetEmulator (netemulator.com).** https://netemulator.com
A web-based network emulator with a drag-and-drop topology builder. Students construct networks from routers, switches, and hosts, then test configurations by sending packets between devices and receiving real-time feedback on errors. The tool visualizes data packet movement through the network. Relevant as an existing browser-based interactive network learning tool. Strength: web-based, no installation required, interactive with real-time packet visualization. Limitation: focused on protocol configuration and troubleshooting (correct interface settings, routing table entries), not on topology optimization or performance scoring. There is no gamification layer (levels, progressive constraints, scoring) and no connection between topology structure and aggregate performance metrics like PFLOPS or all-reduce completion time.

## Common Approaches

The existing work falls into three distinct categories that do not overlap:

1. **Topology research** produces new network designs (fat-tree, dragonfly, Jellyfish) and analyzes their theoretical properties. These papers assume the reader already has deep expertise in graph theory and networking. They do not attempt to build intuition or make the design trade-offs experiential.

2. **Network simulation** provides tools (ns-3, FatTreeSim, Wormhole) for researchers to evaluate topology performance with high fidelity. These are heavyweight research instruments, not educational tools. They require significant expertise to set up and interpret, and they operate at scales far beyond what a learner needs.

3. **Educational gamification** creates interactive tools for learning networking concepts (Conquer the Net, NetEmulator), but these focus on device configuration and protocol behavior, not on the topology design problem itself. No existing tool lets a user design a network topology from scratch, see how it performs under a realistic workload, and compare their design against known optimal solutions.

## Gap Analysis

No existing tool occupies the intersection of all three categories. The gap is:

**There is no interactive, gamified tool that lets users design datacenter network topologies under realistic constraints and receive immediate, quantitative feedback on how their design affects distributed workload performance.**

The topology papers tell you *what* works but not *why* it feels right. The simulators let you *measure* performance but require expert setup. The educational tools teach *configuration* but not *design*. The proposed project fills this gap by combining topology theory, lightweight packet-level simulation, and gamification into a single browser-based experience where the user learns by building, failing, and iterating.
